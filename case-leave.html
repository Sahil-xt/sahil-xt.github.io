Step 1 -- Empathise

The same leave system serves four completely different people.

Leave management in an Indian SME is deceptively complex. On one side, HR admins deal with government compliance, multiple workforce types, and year-end processing chaos. On the other, a blue collar factory worker just wants to know if his leave was approved before salary day. We mapped all four personas through empathy interviews and observations.

Persona 01 -- Owner / Admin
Owner / Admin
Defines company-wide leave policies, monitors compliance
Says
I want different policies to manage the workforce. Policies should be flexible. I need an overview dashboard. Employees don't take leaves during the whole year.
Thinks
What leave types to give? Am I following govt rules? Are rules different for labourers? How are employees taking leaves? Should I restrict rules due to past misuse?
Does
Researches policies. Takes reference from other companies. Delegates to HR. Requests monthly leave reports. Reviews everything is in place.
Feels
Anxious about process going wrong. Excited for better policies. Worried about multiple policy assignment. Frustrated with unauthorised absenteeism.
Pain
  • Difficulty understanding government policies
  • Employees accumulate leaves and take all at year-end
  • Multiple policy assignment confusion
  • Confusion in terminology of rules
  • Frustrated with unauthorised absenteeism
Gain
  • Ready reference of govt policies by state
  • One policy per employee enforced by system
  • Analytics on leave-taking patterns
  • Info tips for each feature with examples
  • Leave summary report as quick access

Key insight from research: The HR manager's year-end processing was the most painful moment in the entire leave lifecycle -- calling every manager to clear requests, manually calculating carry-forward, updating Excel sheets under payroll pressure. This single scenario became the design anchor for several automation features.
Step 2 -- Define

Three distinct problems hiding under one module.

Synthesising across all four personas revealed three separable problem areas, each with a different owner:

  • Policy complexity (HR/Admin): India's leave rules vary by state, industry, and employment type. Creating a policy that satisfies legal requirements, company preferences, and blue-vs-white collar differences -- without a single wrong-click causing a payroll error -- was the central challenge.
  • Approval friction (Manager): Managers saw leave approvals as an interruption to their real work. The system required too many clicks, provided too little context, and sent no proactive reminders -- making approvals consistently late and triggering salary deduction disputes.
  • Transparency gap (Employee): Employees had no visibility into how leave rules applied to them until they were rejected or deducted. The system assumed knowledge they didn't have -- especially blue collar workers unfamiliar with software or English.
HR Admin's expectation from user journey mapping: "Easy way to create a new policy. The process should be flexible. Should be time saving." -- This became our design benchmark for the entire policy creation flow.
Step 3 -- Ideate

Five structural approaches. One question to answer.

The core design question in the policy creation flow: where does employee assignment happen relative to policy creation? Doing both in one flow reduces steps but increases cognitive load. Separating them adds flexibility but risks incomplete setup. We explored five variations and converged on one.

Approach 1
Create and assign in one continuous linear flow
Create Policy-> Add Title-> Define Leave Types + Rules-> Select Employees-> Assign Policy
Pros
One-time activity for create and assign both.
Cons
Overloaded flow. No draft feature. Cannot finish without assigning. Second-time assignment requires edit mode.
Approach 2
Create now, assign as optional later
Create Policy-> Add Title-> Define Leave Types + Rules-> Assign Employee? (optional)-> Save
Pros
Assign is optional -- HR can do it later.
Cons
Still overloaded. No draft feature. More steps in defining leave types and rules.
Approach 3
Merged: employee criteria inside rules section
Create Policy-> Add Title-> Select Employee Criteria-> Define Leave Types + Rules-> Save / Assign
Premise
With audience in mind, HR defines title and rules with more confidence. Goal achieved in one continuous process.
Why not chosen
Rules for probation/tenure-based employees live inside the Rules section -- previewing employees before rules creates a disconnect from the actual goal of policy setup.
Approaches 4A and 4B
Audience-first -- select employees before defining rules
Create Policy-> Add Title-> Select Employees-> Define Leave Types + Rules (per type)-> Save / Assign
Pros
One-time create and assign. Employee criteria can be completed in Step 1 of current process.
Cons
Overloaded. No draft. Cannot finish without assigning. Carries cognitive load of leave types and rules for longer -- until employee criteria is finished.
Chosen approach
Approach 5 -- Rule-based Home Setting
Modular: create policy independently, assign separately as a distinct action
Leave Policy View-> Create Policy-> Add Title + Leave Types-> Save
All Policies View-> Select Policy and Assign-> Select Employee Criteria (max 3 combinations)-> Save / Assign
Why we chose this
Easily adaptable to the current developed process. Less cognitive load -- the whole process can be broken into separate sessions. Edit and assigning can be done separately when the user returns. Draft preserved. Familiar to users who know competitor tools.
Trade-off
Requires completing multiple distinct processes. Slightly less user-friendly for first-time users unfamiliar with the separation.
Why Approach 5 won: HR managers don't always assign policies in the same session they create them. A new policy might be drafted, reviewed by a senior stakeholder, and only then assigned to employees. Keeping create and assign as separate, resumable actions matched the real workflow -- not the ideal one.

Holiday Calendar -- a separate ideation thread

In parallel to policy setup, we mapped the HR admin's mental model for defining the annual holiday calendar. The thought process runs in a predictable order: National and Compulsory holidays first, then Optional ones (preferably near weekly offs), then State-level holidays relevant to company locations. We designed the calendar setup to mirror this exact sequence -- not alphabetically or by date, but by priority type.

Key prototype decisions from research synthesis

  • Default leave types on first entry: Rather than an empty policy, we pre-populated common leave types (CL, PL, SL) so HR could edit rather than start from scratch.
  • Draft save at every step: Because policy creation spans multiple sessions and stakeholder reviews, draft state is preserved automatically without manual save.
  • Maximum 3 employee criteria combinations: Research showed that more than 3 assignment criteria caused confusion and errors. The system enforces this limit with a clear override view.
  • Progressive disclosure per leave type: Rules for each leave type are set individually per screen -- not all on one page -- reducing cognitive overload while allowing deep configuration.
  • Year-end processing automation: Automated reminders to managers, bulk approve/reject option for HR, and auto carry-forward calculation eliminated the most painful parts of year-end.
  • Employee-facing policy summary: When a policy is assigned to an employee, they receive a plain-language summary of their leave entitlements -- not a link to the admin config.

My Info -- the employee UX flow

In parallel to the admin/policy side, we designed the complete employee leave-taking journey -- from the "My Info" dashboard through to application, approval tracking, and balance visibility. Key decisions: a tour guide for new users, leave reminder triggers to managers, and an "apply on behalf" option for hospitalised or non-digital workers.

New employee path

Tour guide triggered on first login. Explains leave types in plain language. Surfaces available balance and pending requests prominently.

Experienced employee path

Direct access to Apply for Leave. Calendar view showing team leaves and holidays. Reminder triggers to manager. Leave cancellation if plans change.

Step 5 -- Test and Ship

Tested with HR managers across industries. Shipped across 1000+ companies.

Usability testing was conducted with HR managers from manufacturing, retail, IT services, and logistics companies. Key findings that changed the design before shipping:

  • Default policy discovery: All HR testers went directly to edit the default policy rather than creating a new one -- validating the "pre-populate on entry" decision and saving significant setup time.
  • Applicable For confusion confirmed: Every tester struggled with the overlap scenario. We added a real-time employee preview that showed exactly who was affected before saving, resolving 80% of the confusion.
  • Year-end automation validated: Automated manager reminders were the single highest-valued feature across all HR testers. "This alone saves me 2 hours of calling every month."
  • Blue collar SMS adoption: Testing with factory supervisors confirmed that SMS for leave status updates was the only viable communication channel -- app notifications and emails were not reliable for blue collar workers.
  • Reports flow -- two paths emerged: Managers wanted quick insights and graphs. HR wanted custom reports they could export and share with owners. We shipped two separate entry points with different defaults.

What shipped

  • Modular policy creation (Approach 5) -- create, configure, and assign as separable independent actions
  • Progressive disclosure per leave type -- rules configured individually per leave type, not all on one screen
  • Year-end automation -- automated manager reminders, bulk HR actions, and auto carry-forward with exception handling
  • Team availability calendar -- employees and managers see team leave dates before applying
  • Employee-facing plain-language policy summary -- communicated at assignment, not buried in admin settings
  • Inline constraint warnings -- limitation rules shown at application time, not after rejection
  • SMS notifications for blue collar workers -- leave approval status, expiry reminders, salary impact warnings
  • Leave cancellation -- employees can cancel approved leaves if plans change, with manager notification
  • Reports with two paths -- insights and graphs for managers, custom export for HR admins
