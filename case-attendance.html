Step 1 — Empathise

Five personas. One system. Completely different realities.

Attendance in an Indian SME touches five very different people — each with entirely different mental models, pressures, and literacy levels. We ran interviews and contextual observation across all five, and mapped what they say, think, do and feel.

Persona 01 — Owner / Admin
Owner / Admin
Defines company-wide attendance policies
Says
I want different policies for different workforce types. Policies should be flexible enough to change in future. I need an overview dashboard with daily updates.
Thinks
How to capture attendance — app or device? How to handle blue vs white collar? What government policies must I follow?
Does
Plans alternate resources when someone is on leave. Regularises attendance manually. Researches policies. Delegates policy work. Monitors reports.
Feels
Anxious about process going wrong. Frustrated with device misuse. Excited for better policies. Worried about multiple policy assignments.
Pain
  • Difficulties understanding government policies
  • No warning on legally non-compliant transactions
  • Proxy attendance and ghost workers
  • No real-time visibility on punctuality
  • Challenges communicating policy to team
  • Multiple policy assignment confusion
Gain
  • Ready reference of govt policies
  • Smart notifications on policy violations
  • Facial recognition + biometric options
  • Quick summary view of punctuality metrics
  • Policy summary communicated to employees
  • Complex rules explained simply

Key insight from empathy mapping: The same attendance system is used by a factory floor worker with no smartphone and a college-educated HR manager running policy from a laptop. Designing for both simultaneously — without compromising either — became the central design challenge.
Step 2 — Define

The problem wasn't attendance. It was policy complexity.

After synthesising across all five personas, the core tension emerged: HR managers needed maximum configurability to handle India's complex attendance rules (grace periods, comp-off, overtime, regularisation windows, sandwich policies) — but that configurability was creating cognitive overload and frequent misconfiguration.

Meanwhile, employees on the ground — especially blue collar workers — had no visibility into how policies affected them until payday, when it was too late.

Key problem statements

  • For HR / Admin: Creating a new attendance policy involves too many disconnected steps, unclear terminologies, and no safety net against misconfiguration.
  • For Managers: No real-time visibility on team absenteeism makes it impossible to plan production or justify overtime costs.
  • For White Collar Employees: No proactive warnings mean salary deductions come as a shock — leading to disputes and loss of trust in the system.
  • For Blue Collar Workers: The system assumes smartphone literacy and language fluency — two things factory floor workers often don't have.
Defining success: HR Admin's stated expectation from a user journey: "Easy way to create a new policy. The process should be flexible. Less time required to complete creation." This became our benchmark for the Define step.
Step 3 — Ideate

Six structural approaches to the same problem.

Attendance policy setup has three core configurable areas: Attendance Settings (high complexity — shift rules, grace periods, overtime), Capturing Options (low complexity — how to record attendance), and Regularisation Policy (medium complexity — correction windows and rules). We explored six structural models for how HR navigates through these.

Approach 1
Linear — all in one policy, sequential steps
Attendance Settings → Regularisation → Capturing Options → Applicable For
Pros
One place to create everything. Draft/save feature. No need for multiple linked policies.
Cons
Multiple steps to complete. Options overloaded. Capturing settings may be same across all employees — doesn't need to be per-policy.
Approach 2
Two-row — attendance separate from capturing
Attendance Settings → Capturing Options → Regularisation + Applicable For
Regularisation Policy → Applicable For
Pros
Minimum steps. Lower cognitive load. Draft feature. Keeps regularisation independent.
Cons
Some rules are shared across policies — defining per policy increases redundancy. Requires multiple linked policies.
Approach 3
Three-row — all three policies independently configurable
Attendance Settings → Select Capturing + Regularisation + Applicable For
Pros
One step creation. Lower cognitive load. Flexible — can assign capturing/regularisation later. Multiple ways to link policies.
Cons
Some rules still shared. Requires multiple policies to be created and linked with attendance.
Approach 4
Compact — attendance + capturing combined, regularisation separate
Attendance Settings → Select Capturing + Regularisation + Applicable For
Pros
One step. Lower cognitive load. Draft feature. Possible to assign regularisation/capturing later.
Cons
Shared rules still need multiple policies. Capturing and regularisation need to be pre-created.
Approach 5
Policy-first — regularisation and capturing independently linked
Attendance Settings → Select Regularisation + Applicable For
Capturing Options → Assign Attendance Policy
Pros
Minimum steps. Lower cognitive load. Draft. Keeping capturing settings independent means HR can configure only capturing without touching full attendance settings.
Cons
Shared rules create redundancy. Must create multiple policies and link them.
Chosen approach
Approach 6
Modular — attendance and capturing as independent but linkable policies
Attendance Settings → Regularisation → Applicable For
Capturing Policy → Attendance Settings or Applicable For
Why we chose this
Minimum steps. Lower cognitive load. Keeping attendance capturing settings independently means HR can configure only capturing methods without having to open and re-save full attendance settings. Draft feature preserved. Flexible linking.
Trade-off
Some rules are still common across policies. Requires creating and linking separate policies for regularisation.
Why six approaches? Each approach represented a different mental model: "Everything in one place" vs "Independent modules" vs "Link as you go." The key insight from evaluating all six was that capturing policy and attendance policy have different update frequencies — capturing (biometric vs geo-fence) changes rarely, while attendance rules (grace periods, overtime) change seasonally. Separating them respects that cadence.

Key prototype decisions

  • Default policy on first entry: Rather than showing an empty state, we pre-populated a default policy so HR could explore and edit rather than start from scratch.
  • Progressive disclosure: Attendance settings (highest complexity) are shown first; capturing options (lowest complexity) are on a separate screen HR can defer.
  • Draft save at every step: Because policy creation spans multiple sessions (reviews, stakeholder approvals), draft state is preserved automatically.
  • Overlap validation: When assigning a policy to a group, the system checks for employees already assigned to another policy and shows a clear conflict resolution UI.
  • One policy per employee: System enforces this — attempting to assign a second policy automatically re-assigns rather than duplicating, with an alert.
Step 5 — Test & Ship

Validated with HR managers. Shipped across 1000+ companies.

We ran usability testing with HR managers from 5 companies across different industries — manufacturing, logistics, retail, IT services, and healthcare. Key findings informed the final design:

  • Default policy discovery: 4/5 HR managers went directly to edit the default policy rather than creating a new one — confirming the "default on entry" decision.
  • Applicable For confusion: All 5 HR managers struggled with the overlap scenario during testing, validating our emotional mapping from the task flow. We added an inline preview of "affected employees" before saving.
  • Blue collar kiosk: Testing with factory floor workers confirmed zero-text, face-in-frame, audio+visual feedback was the only viable interaction model. Even bilingual text caused hesitation.
  • Notification timing: Employees wanted warnings before the payroll cycle closed — not after salary deductions. We added a proactive reminder system triggered 3 days before payroll lock.

What shipped

  • Modular policy creation (Approach 6) — attendance, regularisation, and capturing as separately configurable and linkable policies
  • Face scan kiosk — hardware device with zero-text UI, audio confirmation, and face recognition for factory floor workers with no smartphone
  • Mobile geo-fenced clock-in — for field and office employees with 50m geo-fence precision
  • Exceptions-first admin dashboard — HR sees flagged anomalies first, not a sea of green ticks
  • Proactive notification system — employees notified of anomalies, managers notified on team leave events, HR notified before payroll closes
  • Plain-language attendance summary — policy terms translated into plain language communicated to employees at assignment
