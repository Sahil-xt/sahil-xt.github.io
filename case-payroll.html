Step 1 -- Empathise

Payroll lives at the intersection of anxiety and trust.

For HR managers and accounts managers, payroll is the highest-stakes task in the HRMS cycle -- one wrong value affects every employee. For employees, it is the monthly moment of truth: did the system get it right? We interviewed both sides and built detailed persona profiles grounded in real user research.

Key insight from research: Both HR and Accounts personas said the same thing differently -- HR said "focus only on anomaly employees, not all employees", and Accounts said "arrears is the most frustrating thing to calculate." Both pointed to the same root cause: the system treats every employee equally during payroll run, forcing effort across all 500+ when only a handful have changed. This became the design north star for the pre-run validation flow.
Step 2 -- Define

Three distinct failure modes, each with a different owner.

Payroll has a hard deadline and zero tolerance for error. Synthesising the research revealed four clear failure modes:

HR / Accounts Manager
Pre-run visibility problem
Before running payroll, HR has no way to see which employees have anomalies -- pending attendance, missing declarations, salary revisions -- without checking each one individually. Same effort for all, even when only a few have changed.
HR / Accounts Manager
Statutory complexity problem
PF, ESI, TDS, PT, LWF each have different calculation rules, transfer timelines, and government portals. HR frequently misses transfer deadlines, triggering penalties. Challan generation and return filing involve multiple manual steps with no system guidance.
Employee
Payslip opacity problem
Employees receive payslips with line items they don't understand -- "LOP", "PT", "TDS", "CTC breakup" -- with no plain-language explanation. When deductions change, there's no proactive notification. Result: distrust and repeated HR queries.
All personas
Integration gap problem
Payroll is downstream of attendance and leave data. When attendance records have corrections or leave applications are pending, payroll is affected -- but the connection was invisible, causing surprise deductions and manual reconciliation every month.
Defining the lock moment: Once payroll is finalised, reversing it is extremely difficult and damages employee trust. The design problem is therefore not just about running payroll -- it is about building confidence before the lock happens, so HR can run without anxiety.
Step 3 -- Ideate

Exceptions first. Always.

The most consequential ideation decision was how to structure payroll run initiation. We explored three models:

  • Run-first model: HR runs payroll, then reviews output. Fast to initiate but reactive -- errors discovered after the fact when reversing is costly. Rejected because it doesn't reduce anxiety.
  • Checklist model: A static checklist HR must manually tick before running. Better, but checklists are hard to keep current and don't surface actual anomalies -- only procedural gates.
  • Exceptions-first validation (chosen): Before running, the system automatically surfaces only employees with anomalies -- pending attendance, missing declarations, salary changes, new joiners. HR resolves just those, then runs with confidence.
Why exceptions-first won: Bhavesh's exact quote: "It is better to focus on only those employees rather than same effort for all employees." In a 500-person company, typically 10-30 employees have something to resolve. Surfacing only those saves hours and eliminates "did I miss something?" anxiety.

Payslip: plain language over jargon

For the employee-facing payslip we explored two directions: a traditional finance-formatted payslip (familiar to accountants, opaque to employees) versus a plain-language card design. Research was clear -- employees don't need accountant-grade formatting. They need to know their take-home, what was deducted, and why. We chose plain-language design with expandable detail.

Role-based views: one payroll, four perspectives

We mapped four distinct views from the same payroll data: Owner (cashflow summary), HR Admin (full control + statutory), Manager (team salary for budget planning), Employee (personal payslip + tax details). Designing the information hierarchy for each view separately -- rather than one screen for everyone -- was the key ideation output.

Key prototype decisions

  • Exceptions-only dashboard: Pre-run screen shows only anomaly employees -- not all 500. Exception count by category (attendance, declarations, revisions, new joiners) gives HR a quick workload overview.
  • Inline resolution: Each exception resolved directly on the validation screen without navigating away. Attendance corrections, declaration approvals, and salary confirmations are all inline actions.
  • Lock confirmation with impact preview: Lock screen shows total employee count, total payroll amount, and delta from last month -- one final verification before the irreversible action.
  • Role-specific views: Admin sees full payroll control. HR sees employee-level detail and statutory. Manager sees team salary summary for budget planning. Employee sees personal payslip in plain language.
  • Statutory deadline tracker: PF, ESI, TDS, PT, LWF each show a countdown to due date with one-click challan initiation and pre-filled data.
  • Export compatibility: Payroll exports in Tally, Zoho, and Saral Pay formats -- matching tools Indian SME accountants already use.
Step 5 -- Test and Ship

Tested with HR and Accounts users. Validated before lock.

Usability testing conducted with HR managers and accounts managers from 4 companies across manufacturing, IT services, retail, and logistics. Key findings that changed the design before shipping:

  • Exception count matters before the list: All testers wanted to know how many exceptions existed before opening the validation screen. We added a categorised count summary on the payroll home card.
  • Investment proof validation was the biggest pain: Testers needed to see proof documents inline, not in a separate upload portal. We integrated the document viewer directly into the validation flow.
  • Lock confirmation was the most feared moment: Every tester paused significantly at the lock screen. We added a final summary with employee count, total payout, and last-month variance plus an explicit confirmation step.
  • Employees wanted tax impact visibility: Employees asked to see how investment declarations would affect TDS before submitting. We added a live tax impact calculator in the declaration flow.
  • Statutory deadline notifications immediately validated: All HR testers said they had missed statutory transfer deadlines at least once. The deadline countdown with direct action was rated the highest-value new feature.
