Step 1 -- Empathise

Surveillance anxiety is real -- and rational.

Field employees -- sales reps, service technicians, delivery staff -- reacted strongly when they learned their location would be tracked. "My company will know where I am every minute. What if GPS shows me somewhere wrong? What if I stop for tea?" These weren't irrational fears. They reflected real experiences with opaque systems that offered no way to verify or dispute the data.

Key insight from research: The transparency principle emerged from a single conversation with a field sales rep: "I don't mind being tracked if I can see what they see." Once we made employee-visible tracking the design principle -- not the exception -- every other design decision followed naturally from it.
Step 2 -- Define

Give employees the same view their manager has.

The defining design decision came directly from research: if employees could see their own location data -- the same view their manager sees -- resistance would drop significantly. Transparency, not restriction, was the answer. From this, three specific problem statements emerged:

Field Employee
Transparency problem
Employees are tracked but have no visibility into what data is recorded or what their manager sees. This opacity creates anxiety, distrust, and resistance to adoption -- even among employees with nothing to hide.
Field Employee
Expense disconnection problem
Travel expenses are submitted separately from visit records -- often at end of day or end of week from memory. This leads to missing claims, wrong amounts, and HR spending time reconciling expenses against visit data manually.
Field Manager
Real-time visibility problem
Managers receive end-of-day reports only. They cannot make territory planning decisions, handle emergencies, or redirect team members based on current field positions. The data is always 8+ hours stale.
HR / Admin
GPS accuracy problem
In poor-signal areas (basements, rural locations, dense urban), GPS coordinates are inaccurate by 100m or more. With no correction mechanism, employees cannot dispute wrong location data -- creating distrust and HR escalations.
The transparency principle as design north star: Every feature decision was tested against one question: "Does this give employees more visibility into their own data, or less?" Features that failed this test were dropped or redesigned.
Step 3 -- Ideate

Visit timeline + expense linking in one flow.

Two major ideation threads ran in parallel -- the employee mobile experience and the manager web dashboard -- with one shared constraint: the data shown to both must be identical.

Employee app: three models explored

  • Passive tracking (rejected): App tracks location in background continuously. Simplest for employee but creates "I'm always being watched" anxiety. No employee agency. Rejected.
  • Manual check-in/check-out (chosen direction): Employee explicitly checks in when arriving at a client location and checks out on leaving. Employee has agency and awareness. Creates a natural moment to attach expenses at checkout.
  • Hybrid with geo-fence trigger (considered): App prompts check-in when employee enters a registered client location's geo-fence. Reduces forget-to-check-in problem but requires pre-registration of all client addresses. Implemented as an optional enhancement.

Expense linking: the checkout moment

Research showed expense submission rates were dramatically lower when it was a separate step. The ideation insight: checkout is the natural moment of completion -- the employee is leaving the location and mentally closing the visit. Attaching expense submission to checkout captured expenses at the moment of highest context and motivation.

Manager dashboard: map vs list

Map-first view

Live map showing team positions. Good for territory planning and real-time dispatch. Poor for reviewing visit history or comparing employee performance.

Timeline-first view (chosen)

Chronological visit history per employee with map context on demand. Better for review workflows, expense approval, and performance analytics. Map embedded as secondary view.

Why manual check-in won over passive tracking: In testing, employees with manual check-in reported 40% lower anxiety scores than those with passive tracking -- even though the data collected was similar. The act of checking in gave employees a sense of control and transparency.

Key prototype decisions

  • Employee-visible tracking: Employees see their own complete visit timeline -- same data as manager. No hidden data. This single decision drove adoption more than any other feature.
  • Expense linking at check-out: A slide-up sheet at check-out prompts expense entry in context. Expense capture rate increased dramatically vs separate submission flow.
  • Manual location correction: When GPS is inaccurate, employee can flag the location and enter the correct address. Creates an audit trail of corrections visible to both employee and manager.
  • GPS fallback strategy: In poor-signal areas, the last known accurate position is retained with a timestamp, and employees are prompted to manually confirm or correct location at check-in.
  • Work-hours-only tracking: Location tracking activates only when the employee starts a trip and deactivates on end trip -- never outside work context.
Step 5 -- Test and Ship

Transparency worked exactly as predicted.

Post-test interviews with field employees who could see their own data showed significantly lower resistance compared to those using a manager-only visibility model. Key usability testing findings:

  • Employee-visible data reduced anxiety by ~40%: In moderated testing, employees who saw their own timeline data reported feeling "monitored but not surveilled" -- a key distinction that drove willingness to use the app daily.
  • Expense linking at check-out captured 3x more expenses: Compared to the previous separate submission flow, inline expense linking at check-out captured significantly more claims with less friction.
  • GPS fallback was critical for factory and rural users: In areas with poor signal, employees without a correction mechanism felt "trapped by wrong data." The manual correction option was rated the second highest-value feature after transparency itself.
  • End-of-day summary became the most used screen: Employees opened the day summary to verify their record before logging off -- an unplanned usage pattern that reinforced the transparency design.

What shipped

  • Employee-visible location tracking -- employees see the same visit history their manager sees
  • Manual check-in/check-out -- employee agency and awareness, not passive background tracking
  • Expense linking at check-out -- slide-up expense entry in context of the visit, not separately
  • Manual location correction -- employees can flag GPS errors with an audit trail
  • GPS fallback for poor-signal areas -- last known position retained, manual confirmation prompted
  • Work-hours-only tracking -- location data only during active trip, never outside work context
  • Timeline-first manager dashboard -- chronological visit history with map context on demand
  • Day summary notification -- end-of-day push summary for employee self-verification
